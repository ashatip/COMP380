Gyroscopes

A spatial orientation sensor built on the principles of rotational motion

What are they?

Gyroscopes are sensors consisting of a very small rotating wheel on three axes. As the wheel rotates, it maintains its orientation in space because of the conservation of angular momentum and as such can be used in addition to an accelerometer to understand a users orientation and motion in space. Gyroscopes in consumer electronic devices were originally introduced by Apple's iPhone and have since been included in mobile devices of all forms.

The gyroscopes commonly used in electronic devices are known as microelectromechanical systems gyroscopes or MEMS gyroscopes. These MEMS gyroscopes are composed of varying structures consisting of either resonators, vibrating wheels, or tuning forks in conjunction with piezolectric components which convert mechanical motions into electric signals which can be used by the larger device.

Research uitlizing MEMS Gyroscopes

Gyroscopes are of interest to medical researchers, particularly their applications in tracking the movements of a patient's joints or limbs in those members of the elderly population who are more threatened by falls and stumbles. As such, it is important to monitor the stability and gait of elderly folks. A 2011 study done by Ishigaki, et. al in the Journal of Biomechanics investigates this issue by placing a triaxial gyroscope and accelerometer on participants hip joints in order to track the angle, angular velocity, and acceleration of their joints. Participants were grouped into a stable group and an unstable group based on a visual assessment of their posture and measurement of the flexibility in their legs. Then participants were instructed to walk 10 meters tracking their hip movements with the gyroscope. It was found that older participants tended to more often be grouped in the unstable group and have less confidence in the motions of their hips which swung in much smaller angles and at slower speeds for an overall lack of pelvic movement.
