SOCIAL IMPLICATIONS

Your Concerns Are Valid And We Don't Plan To Hide Them

The general collection of data is a device-specific privacy concern caused by the fact that the data collected is not solely owned by the user. Facial recognition in another device-specific privacy concern that is starting to become integrated into devices. With the right algorithms, this ID technology will soon become widely available and pose a privacy concern for those who wish not to be seen. Social media synchronization is common in devices. Posting on one outlet usually results in other social media sites accessing that information as well. This automatic synchronization is not always a pro for users that own wearables. GPS tracking and discloser is a common feature in wearables that poses a sensor-specific privacy concern. With this feature, finding ones location through wearables just became a whole lot easier. The audio our wearables capture is another sensor-specific privacy concern. Depending on the specific device, the user may be unaware that audio is captured. Many users are concerned with the spying capabilities of wearable technology. This device-specific privacy concern stems from individuals fearing others may gain access to the data recorded or viewed on the wearable. The ability to capture data and share that content to other platforms is a very convenient amenity. However, sometimes users may wish to not have certain captured data published or feel the need to remove the data at a later point. While the user might feel safe showing off their face or other information to the world, the people around them may not feel the same way. Individuals around the person using a wearable have not granted consent to for their data collection.



Vivian Genaro Motti & Kelly Caine wrote an extensive study regarding Usersâ€™ Privacy Concerns About Wearables. These two authors complete a observational, research study that surveyed individuals about their concerns with wearable technology. They discoverd four major types of privacy concerns: device-specific, sensor-specific, data-specific, and application-specific. Their underlying argument in their study stems from the fact that, "Not only because wearable computers are able to sense, process, and store intimate information about the users, but also because wearables are able to do it continuously and discretely." The idea behind this concept is that wearables define one more layer that takes away an individual's privacy, and in this case, many times without the user realizing they relinquishing their privacy.
