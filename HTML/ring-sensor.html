MIT's Ring Sensor

A research project in creating comfortable, persistent heart monitoring

The Ring Sensor and Photoplethysmograms

The photoplethysmographic ring sensor is a unique invention from MIT based on the principles of pulse oximeters, medical devices worn on the fingers of patients to monitor their pulse, oxygen levels, and blood volume in a non-invasive way. Pulse oximeters work by passing wavelengths of light through a persons finger or ear lobe to a photodetector on the other side of the limb. The device then measures the differences in absorbance between each wavelength, computing the absorbances due to pulsing arterial blood. While these are effective in hospital settings, some patients with chronic cardiovascular issues require long term ambulatory monitoring in order to aid the treatment of conditions like high blood pressure, and obesity which may lead to cardiovasuclar disease.

The ring sensor is thus an effort to design a wearable sensor which comfortably and non-invasively monitors a patient's cardiovascular health. The device must have a small battery and remain unaffected by the users movement and transmit data to a computer phone or other means. LED and photo detectors are placed on opposite lateral sides of the finger so that light absorption in order to optimize for accuracy of measurement. In order to account for the high consumption of electricity by lights that are always on, researchers decided upon the use of high speed LED's and photodetectors which only need to turn on for miniscule fractions of a second. After the development of several prototypes, Asada et. al arrive to the conclusion that the photoplethysmographic ring sensor is useful in applications ranging from catastrophe detection, chronic medical conditions which require reminders to take medications, hypertension, and congestive heart failure.
