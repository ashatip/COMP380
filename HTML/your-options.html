Sensors, Textiles and Their Wearable Applications

Sensors

Wearable devices rely on various sensors tracking aspects of a users body such as pulse, temperature, blood pressure, physical location, and spatial orientation to create data. Some of the sensors commonly found in health centric wearables are accelerometers, gyroscopes, strain gauges, piezolectric materials, fibre optics, and pressure sensors. Each of these sensors gathers a different type of data and are examined in detail below.

Accelerometers

Accelerometers are small sensors found in most modern portable electronic devices since the iPhone. Accelerometers capture data about the movement of a device, using this information to accurately measure steps like a pedometer or detect the orientation of a device.

The accelerometer itself can be understood conceptually as a mass on a spring, when the device moves, the accelerometer the mass is displaced to a point that is the result of an acceleration of the casing. Thus the masses displacement is then used to calculate the acceleration of the device. Because the accelrometer is a mechanical device, other sensors must come in to play to convert the mechanical signal into an electric signal. As a result most accelerometers use piezoelectric, piezoresistive and capacitive components which convert motion into electric signal a device can use. While most acclerometers utilise one mass-spring system, multiple mass spring systems can be incorporated in one device to capture acclerations in more than one axis, three mass spring systems being neccessary to capture acclerations in three dimensions.

While accelerometers are commonly found in consumer devices, they also have some medicine specific applications, including the afformentioned pedometer. Accelerometers can be used in CPR applications, measuring the depth of chest compressions given to a patient using pads which contain accleerometers. One such company who implements this in their automatic external defribillator's is Zoll. In addition, for physical training accelerometers can be used to measure strike force on a pad or on a helmet worn during full contact sports to give doctors a better idea of what level of injury they are diagnosing. Pads like these can be beneficial to those doctor's who specialize in sports injuries related to concussions.

Gyroscopes

Gyroscopes are sensors consisting of a very small rotating wheel on three axes. As the wheel rotates it maintains its orientation in space because of the conservation of angular momentum and as such can be used in addition to an accelerometer to understand a users orientation and motion in space. Gyroscopes in consumer electronic devices were originally introduced by Apple's iPhone and have since been included in mobile devices of all forms.

The gyroscopes commonly used in electronic devices are known as microelectromechanical systems gyroscopes or MEMS gyroscopes. These MEMS gyroscopes are composed of varying structures consisting of either resonators, vibrating wheels, tuning forks, and piezolectric components which convert mechanical motions into electric signals which can be used by the larger device.

Gyroscopes are of interest to medical reserearchers, particularly their applications in tracking the movements of a patients joints or limbs in those members of the elderly population who are more threatened by falls and stumbles. As such it is important to monitor the stability and gait of elderly folks. A 2011 study done by Ishigaki, et. al in the Journal of Biomechanics investigates this issue by placing a triaxial gyroscope and accelerometer on participants hip joints in order to track the angle, angular velocity, and acceleration of their joints. Participants were grouped into a stable group and an unstable group based on a visual assessment of their posture and measurement of the flexibility in their legs. Then participants were instructed to walk 10 meters tracking their hip movements with the gyroscope. It was found that older participants tended to more often be grouped in the unstable group and have less confidence in the motions of their hips which swung in much smaller angles and at slower speeds for an overall lack of pelvic movement.

Ishigaki, Norio. “Analysis of Pelvic Movement in the Elderly during Walking Using a Posture Monitoring System Equipped with a Triaxial Accelerometer and a Gyroscope.” Journal of Biomechanics, vol. 44, no. 9, June 2011, pp. 1788–1792., doi:10.1016/j.jbiomech.2011.04.016.

The Ring Sensor and Photoplethysmograms

The photoplethysmographic ring sensor is a unique invention from MIT based on the principles of pulse oximeters, medical devices worn on the fingers of patients to monitor their pulse, oxygen levels, and blood volume in a non-invasive way. Pulse oximeters work by passing wavelengths of light through a persons finger or ear lobe to a photodetector on the other side of the limb. The device then measures the differences in absorbance between each wavelength, computing the absorbances due to pulsing arterial blood. While these are effective in hospital settings, some patients with chronic cardiovascular issues require long term ambulatory monitoring in order to aid the treatment of conditions like high blood pressure, and obesity which may lead to cardiovasuclar disease.

The ring sensor is thus an effort to design a wearable sensor which comfortabley and non-invasively monitors a patient's cardiovascular health. The device must have a small battery and remain unaffected by the users movement and transmit data to a computer phone or other means. LED and photo detectors are placed on opposite lateral sides of the finger so that light absorption in order to optimize for accuracy of measurement. In order to account for the high consumption of electricity by lights that are always on, researchers decided upon the use of high speed LED's and photodetectors which only need to turn on for miniscule fractions of a second. After the development of several prototypes, Asada et. al arrive to the conclusion that the photoplethysmographic ring sensor is useful in applications ranging from catastrophe detection, chronic medical conditions which require reminders to take medications, hypertension, and congestive heart failure.
